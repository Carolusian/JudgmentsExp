140. As indicated in the early part of this judgment the plaintiffâs claim against the 3rd defendant is for fraudulent misrepresentation.

142. It is now necessary to consider the law to ascertain whether the plaintiffâs claim can succeed.

143. The law on the Tort of Fraudulent Misrepresentation is conveniently set out in Chapter 18 of the 20th Edition of Clerk & Lindsell on Torts, Sweet & Maxwell.

144. The definition is contained in 18-01 :

â Definition The modern development of the tort of deceit (sometimes called simply âfraudâ) dates from Pasley v Freeman in 1789. There, the defendant falsely represented to the claimant that a third party was creditworthy when he knew he was not; theclaimant suffered loss as a result of extending credit to him. The claimant was held to have an action. The tort involves a perfectlygeneral principle : where a defendant makes a false representation, knowing it to be untrue, or being reckless as whether it is true,and intends that the claimant should act in reliance on it, then in so far as the latter does so and suffers loss the defendant isliable. Each aspect of the tort is discussed in detail below.â

145. Having regard to the circumstances of this case the commentary at para. 18-08 is helpful :

â Active conduct or concealment Active non-verbal conduct can amount to misrepresentation, and hence deceit, just as much as words can. So, for instance, pledginggoods knowing one has no title to them is deceit, as is ordering goods on credit for someone known to be insolvent or presentingcompany accounts to a buyer in the knowledge that they have been doctored. No doubt the same would apply to an agent knowingly contractingwithout authority to do so. Another straightforward example is positive steps taken to conceal defects in something being sold (asagainst merely keeping silent about them). So in Gordon v Selico Ltd, the Court of Appeal awarded damages for deceit where a defendant fraudulently arranged to cover up infestations of dry rot in aflat before letting it. Similarly, in the antique case of Schneider v Heath a seller of a ship was held liable when he deliberately floated it so as to hide sub-waterline defects when the buyer came to inspectit.

146. In the present case it is not so much what the 3rd defendant said as what he did. By signing the various documents he was clearly representing that he was authorised to do so andthat the terms and conditions contained in the documents would be complied with.

By signing ithe represented that he was authorised to do so and that the company would comply with the terms and conditions contained in the documents. The plaintiff granted facilities to IAHK to its detriment and loss.

149. The 3rd representation made by the 3rd defendant was the signing of the IA Guarantee dated 25 June 2002 when the decision was made to transfer the facilities extended toIAHK to the 1st defendant. Similar consequences flowed from this as from the first two representations.

It was as a result of this that the facilities were extended by the plaintiff to the 1st defendant.
