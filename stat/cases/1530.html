INFRINGEMENT OF REGISTERED TRADE MARKS

24. Validity of the Trade Marks

25. Mr. Ling submitted that the registration of the Trade Marks were invalid. The essential submissions put forward by Mr. Ling werethat the Trade Marks contained the word âBRATZâ as their core or sole element and that the word âBRATZâ was a play on theword âBRATSâ which, in its ordinary English meaning, meant âa childâ and especially âan ill-behaved childâ. He submittedthat the word âBRATZâ contained an obvious and direct reference to the character of the goods and the word âBRATZâ couldbe used to designate the characteristic of the Plaintiffâs dolls namely, to describe their image as ill-behaved children, an objectionunder section 11(1)(c) of the Trade Marks Ordinance (âthe Ordinanceâ). Finally, he submitted that the Trade Marks were devoid of any distinct character, an objection under section 11(1)(b) of the Ordinance.

26. Section 80 of the Ordinance provides that :-

âIn any proceedings for rectification of the register, the registration of a person as owner of a trade mark shall be prima facieevidence of the validity of the original registration and of any subsequent or other transmission of it.â

27. Hence, the burden is on the Defendants to show that the Trade Marks are invalid.

28. Having considered the submissions put forward by Mr. Ling and Mr. Yan, I am unable to agree to Mr. Lingâs submissions that theregistration of the Trade Marks are invalid.

29. In âDoublemintâ case [2004] RPC 18, Advocate General Jacobs said the followings :-

â A18 In BABY-DRY, the interpretation of Art.7(1)(c) of the Regulations (which is equivalent to section 11(1)(c) of the Ordinance)was in issue in the context of a mark having features in some ways comparable to those of âDoublemintâ. Following a refusalof registration as a Community trade mark, the Court of First Instance essentially confirmed the Board of Appealâs view that sincethe words âbabyâ and âdryâ can both be used to describe characteristics of babiesâ nappies, a mark consisting of nothingother than those words cannot by virtue of Art.7(1)(c), be registered for such goods.

A19 In my Opinion in the ensuing appeal to the Court of Justice, I took the view, first, that a Community trade mark may include descriptive terms but may not consist exclusively of them. I then considered that the brand name âBaby-Dryâ contained elements additional to the descriptive terms âbabyâ and âdryâ:extreme ellipsis, unusual structure and resistance to any intuitive grammatical analysis that would make the meaning immediatelyclear. In addition, âBaby-Dryâ was an invented term and as such less likely to be used descriptively in trade and could moreoverallude to many very different types of product, lessening its descriptiveness in relation to babiesâ nappies. Failure to takesuch factors into account was an error in law.

A20 In its judgment the Court stated that the purpose of Art.7(1)(c) was to prevent registration of signs or indications which,being no different from the usual way of designating the relevant goods or services or their characteristics, could not fulfil thefunction of identifying the undertaking that markets them. Such signs and indications were those which may serve in normal usagefrom a consumerâs point of view to designate those goods or services, directly or by reference to an essential characteristic. A mark composed of such signs or indications should not be refused if it included other signs or indications or if the purely descriptivesigns or indications were presented or configured in a manner that distinguished the resultant whole from the usual way of designatingthe goods or services concerned or their essential characteristics.â

30. In my view, similar to the mark âBABY-DRYâ in the âDoublemintâ case in the context of trade mark registration, âBRATZâis not devoid of any distinctive character under section 11(1)(b) of the Ordinance. In particular, in respect of the trade markâBRATZ and deviceâ with Trade Mark Registration no.200213329, it is crystal clear that the mark does not consist exclusively of a descriptive element (even assuming that âBRATZâ is descriptive of the goods in respect of which the mark is registered,which I do not consider to be the case and this will be dealt with later). The mark clearly consists of other elements such as theparticular manner in which the mark is represented and the ring device.

31. Further, while there is a dispute between parties as to the image of the Plaintiffâs Dolls represented â ill-behaved or rebelliousyoung girls as suggested by the Defendants as opposed to hit and cool young adolescent or teenage girls as suggested by the Plaintiff,I consider that the proper question to be considered when considering whether the registration of a mark is objectionable under section11(1)(c) of the Ordinance is whether it is descriptive of the goods in respect of which the mark is registered. The question is, however, not whether the mark is descriptive of that which is represented by the goods in respect of which the mark is registered. Even if I accept that the word âBRATZâ is a play of the word âBRATSâ, it mayonly be descriptive of children or girls, but clearly not of dolls or dolls accessories.

32. Hence, the registration of the Trade Marks cannot be invalid for the aforesaid reasons and there is no triable issue in this regard.

PASSING OFF

52. The other cause of action against the Defendants is passing off. Mr. Yan has helpfully summarized the law regarding the tort ofpassing off. The three essential elements which must be established by a plaintiff are as follows :-

(a) that the plaintiff enjoys reputation and goodwill in the name, mark or indicia which it wishes to prevent the defendant fromusing;

(b) that the defendant has made a misrepresentation which was likely to lead members of the public to believe that his business,goods or services are the business, goods or services of the plaintiff; and

(c) that the plaintiff was suffering and was likely to suffer damage by reason of the erroneous belief engendered by the defendantâsmisrepresentation.

Did the Plaintiff enjoy reputation and goodwill in the name, mark or indicia which it wished to prevent the Defendants from using?

53. Mr. Ling submitted that it was only less than two months later than the launch of âBRATZâ dolls in Hong Kong that the 1st Defendant sold the 1,842 Infringing Dolls to Union Top and therefore there were triable issues as to :-

(1) whether âBRATZâ had by then become sufficiently well-known amongst members of the purchasing public; and

(2) whether the mark was sufficiently distinctive despite its descriptive content so as to distinguish the Plaintiffâs goods fromother similar goods in the minds of the purchasing public.

54. In respect of (1), I do not consider that there exists such triable issue for the following reasons.

55. In the first place, the âBRATZâ dolls were promoted and sold not only in Hong Kong but worldwide. They have won numerous awardsin the toys industry. They also have their own website which is accessible worldwide. Their reputation and goodwill in Hong Kongcould not have just started to be built up after they first sold in Hong Kong. This is supported by the newspaper article of SingTao Daily which was issued on 13th December 2002, a date before the âBRATZâ dolls were first sold in Hong Kong. The article mentioned that the âBRATZâ dollswere the best selling dolls in the United States of America. Photographs and description of the âBRATZâ dolls were given inthe article.

56. Secondly, even if assuming the goodwill and reputation of âBRATZâ dolls only started to be built up after its launch in HongKong (which I do not consider to be the case), I still find that they had built up sufficient goodwill and reputation by the timethe 1st Defendant sold and/or supplied the Infringing Dolls to Union Top.

57. In this regard, it should be noted that it is established law that it is possible for goodwill in a new kind of business to be builtup in a short period of time. In Stannard v. Reay [1967] RPC 589 at 592, Buckley J. said that :-

âAs regards the goodwill which the plaintiffs did or did not have at the time when the defendants started their fish and chip businessin November 1966, Mr. Monckton says with great force that the plaintiffs then had only been trading for some three weeks, or perhapsrather more if one does not take the defendantâs business as effectively under way until rather later than 5th November 1966, and that that would be a very short time in which to acquire a goodwill of any value connected with a trade name. It would seem from the evidence that probably the plaintiffs were the only people in the Isle of Wight carrying on this sort oftrade in this sort of way at the time when they began their business in October 1966. There is no suggestion that there were anyother mobile fish and chip vans then operating in the Isle of Wight, and, although a period of three, four or five weeks may be ashort time in which to build up a goodwill associated with a particular trade name, it does not seem to me to be by any means impossibleto suppose that amongst those members of the public who enjoy fish and chips and who find it convenient to be able to buy them readycooked and supplied at convenient places where they can go and collect them a new business of this kind might not attract to itselfconsiderable notice and trade within quite a short time, and I think that there is to some extent supported by the evidence of thefirst plaintiff to the effect that in the first complete week of trading after the plaintiffâs had put their fish and chip vanto the road, that is to say, the week ending 26th November 1966, the takings of the fish and chip van amounted to ï¿¡129 7s 6d., and in the next week to ï¿¡138 16. 10d., and in thethird week to ï¿¡133 12s. 3Â½d., amounts which seem to me to be substantial sums to be taken by one van in a business of this kind.â

58. The newspaper articles showed that the Plaintiffâs âBRATZâ dolls earned substantial reputation and goodwill by January 2003.
